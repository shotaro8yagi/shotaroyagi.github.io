Why Both?

Both my work and my research take root in the same question: So what is a project, anyway?

We all think we know what a project is, until we try to explain it without relying on managerial terms. I will not unpack this illusion fully here, but it is worth noticing how quickly our language defaults to management concepts.

Goal, deadline, team. These do not explain what project is. They rather express how we want to manage a project.

When projects stall, we often turn to will as an explanation. Will is not a management concept, or so we might think. Hannah Arendt offers a useful pause here.

...the faculty of the Will was unknown to Greek antiquity and was discovered as a result of experiences about which we hear next to nothing before the first century of the Christian era.

—Arendt, H. (1978). The life of the mind (Vol. 2, Willing). Harcourt Brace Jovanovich. p.3

Arendt reminds us that the concept of will is historically produced, not a timeless human faculty.

Management science is full of concepts that feel self-evident and thereby dull our awareness of what is actually happening. This is why we need to draw upon philosophy and sociology to articulate what project is.

There is so much we don't know about how collective action comes together. As Michael Tomasello writes:

Humans putting their heads together in shared cooperative activities are thus the originators of human culture. How and why all of this arose in human evolution is unknown, but one speculation is that in the context of foraging for food (both hunting and gathering), humans were forced to become cooperators in a way that other primates were not.

—Tomasello, M. (2009). Why we cooperate. MIT Press. p.99

Here, a renowned researcher on collaboration is saying that we do not yet know why we came to be collaborative. If he can be humble enough to admit that, why can't we do the same?

I deliberately work as both a practitioner and a researcher in order to articulate what practitioners already sense, but have not yet named.
